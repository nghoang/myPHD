Best Medic Competition winners named


By Michelle Tan - Staff writer
Posted : Monday Oct 29, 2012 12:35:09 EDT

CAMP BULLIS, Texas – Two staff sergeants representing the Western Regional Medical Command won the 2012 Best Medic Competition, which concluded here Sunday.

Staff Sgt. Andrew Balha and Staff Sgt. Alexander Folsom, both Afghanistan veterans, beat out 31 other two-soldier teams during the grueling 72-hour competition to be named the Army’s best medics.

Balha, of Fort Carson, Colo., and Folsom, of Joint Base Lewis-McChord, Wash., each received the Meritorious Service Medal, the combat medic statue, and other prizes for their efforts.

“I was really surprised,” Balha, 32, said about being named the winner. “I didn’t think it would be us.”

Folsom, 26, who competed in last year’s competition with a different partner and finished 10th, said this year’s competition was tougher.

“This one was much more physically demanding, and it had more medical tasks,” he said.

Representing the 2nd Infantry Division, 1st Lt. Jonathan Jordan and Spc. Brandon Chavez earned second place. They received the Army Commendation Medal.

In third place was a team from the 65th Medical Brigade – Sgt. Fernando Diaz and Pfc. Joshua Yi. Diaz and Yi each was awarded the Army Achievement Medal.

The Best Medic Competition included a physical fitness challenge, an obstacle course, pistol and rifle stress shoots, a written exam, and advanced land navigation. Competitors also had to complete combat medic lanes, which tested their ability to perform casualty care in close quarters and evacuate the wounded. They also had to show their medical skills during mass casualty scenarios.

The Best Medic Competition used to be known as the Expert Field Medical Challenge. This was the second year the event has taken on its current form and its new name.

The competition, named after Command Sgt. Maj. Jack L. Clark Jr., is modeled after the Best Ranger Competition, which is notorious for being probably the most challenging and grueling competition in the Army.

Clark served as the top enlisted soldier for Medical Command beginning in February 2003 and was later diagnosed with brain cancer. He died June 17, 2004. He was 51.

To compete in the competition, enlisted soldiers must be in a medical military occupational specialty, and officers must have a medical area of concentration or be a medevac pilot. They also must have earned the Combat Medic Badge or the Expert Field Medical Badge.

On Sunday, Lt. Gen. Patricia Horoho, the Army surgeon general and commanding general of Medical Command, applauded the competitors’ dedication and service.

“You do heroic work each and every day,” Horoho said, as she presided over the awards ceremony. “You make Army medicine proud.”

Combat medics play a vital role on the battlefield, and the Army continues to “train and expand the capabilities of medics,” she said.

“We must do all that’s necessary to make sure our soldiers, airmen, sailors and Marines come home, and come home safely,” she said.
