How to Become an Army Combat Medic

3 authors | 4 revisions | Last updated: June 20, 2012

Judithavory, Nicklepicklepie, BR
Pin It
Article
Edit Discuss

An army combat medic provides medical treatment to wounded troops in active service, whether that's during training or on the battlefield, as well as administrating cases and assisting with inpatient and outpatient treatment. Becoming an army combat medic involves completing basic training in the army before continuing with advanced training to learn the necessary medical skills. An army combat medic is expected to have a sincere interest in caring for others, be an effective communicator, and able to perform his duties meticulously, even in the most stressful and challenging conditions. Read the following steps to learn how to become an army combat medic.

Edit Steps

  1. 1
    Speak to an army recruiter about enlisting. Age, citizenship, marital status and number of dependents, physical fitness, education, and criminal history are factors the army considers before accepting you.
  2. 2
    Complete your basic training. This lasts 9 weeks, during which time you'll receive instructions in basic soldiering skills so you know how to handle yourself during combat and provide cover fire in the event of an attack.
  3. 3
    Continue with your advanced training to become an army combat medic.
    • The purpose of the advanced training is to become skilled at emergency medical techniques, patient care techniques, equipment sterilization and field sanitation methods, and plaster casting techniques. In addition, you'll be trained in how to accompany patrols on their missions, plan and implement en route life support for evacuations, and prescribe preventative and clinical medication. This enables you to provide initial medical treatment to wounded troops at the site of the injury, as well as during transport to healthcare facilities and throughout the subsequent hospital stay.
    • You'll also learn how to train non-medic troops to be combat lifesavers. These troops basically function as a bridge between troops and army combat medics and know how to use basic medical emergency skills, such as basic casualty evaluation, airway management, and bleeding control in order to provide first-aid while waiting for an army combat medic to become available.
    • The civilian equivalent to an army combat medic is an emergency medical technician-basic. Army combat medics can use their medical training to pursue careers in the civilian medical field after their discharge, and many go on to become registered nurses, healthcare administrators, or even doctors in their civilian lives.
  4. 4
    Go through your chain of command to find out how long you need to serve in your current position and what additional training you need if you want to assume more responsibilities as an advanced level health care specialist. In addition to training and supervising other army combat medics, you'll also be able to provide a wider range of emergency and regular medical treatment to troops who are harmed or sick due to combat or other causes; provide assistance with outpatient treatment; and supervise medical facilities both in the field and in clinical settings under the auspices of a physician, physician's assistant, or registered nurse.
