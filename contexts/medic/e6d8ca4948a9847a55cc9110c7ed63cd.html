Overview

MEDIC has been set up to address the gaps in the medical device concept to commercialisation continuum.

MEDIC has three areas of focus and the growth strategy for MEDIC will result in a diverse set of skills capable of bringing a product / technology idea from concept through to design transfer. Product / Technology design transfer will be to a commercialisation entity - external company or Campus Company.

MEDIC's long term growth strategy will be achieved by developing the following capabilities:

  1. Research expertise in the areas of Smart Devices, Assistive Rehabilitation and Biomaterials
  2. Process development - through technology and product concept development as well as innovation partner ships with local companies
  3. Concept development engineering
  4. Medical device product development and commercialisation
  5. Technical and clinical project management

In addition to the skill sets within the MEDIC team, we leverage other expertise as needed in areas such legal (IP), regulatory, commercialisation assessment and product clinical application.

The combination of internal and external skills provides the complete spectrum of expertise  required to bring a product / technology from concept to commercialisation.

She received her PhD in Biomechanical Engineering from National University of Ireland, Galway in 2005. Her graduate work investigated the biomechanical behavior of the thoracolumbar spine using Finite Element Analysis (FEA). She has developed and investigated computationally and experimentally the mechanical behaviour of various bone structures including femur, pelvis, tibia, thoracolumbar spine and scaffolds for bone tissue engineering.

Davida O’Brien
PhD Student

Davida O’Brien graduated from Cork Institute of Technology in 2009 with a Bachelor (hons) of Engineering in Biomedical Engineering. She joined the MEDIC team in September 2009 as a PhD candidate.  As part of her degree, Davida had a paper published entitled “Evaluation of a novel radiopacifiying agent on the physical properties of surgical spineplex” in Journal of Material Science: Materials in Medicine. 
Davida has a keen interest in biomechanics and is pursuing research in this field. Her project investigates the effect of Diabetes on ankle and foot biomechanics. Lower extremity amputation as a result of ulceration is a feared complication among diabetics. Ulceration begins with the onset of peripheral neuropathy (nerve damage) which is linked to muscle wastage, gait misalignment and limited joint mobility. These symptoms along with incresed body weight contribute to elevated plantar (sole of the foot) pressures, leading to ulceration as seen in the picture.
The aim of this study is to examine the kinetics and kinematics of the healthy foot in comparison to the Diabetic foot. These will be investigated through performing analysis on plantar pressure distribution, as seen in Figure 2 (Tekscan HR Mattm , Tekscan F-Scan®), lower limb muscle activity ( Zero Wire EMG ) and gait analysis (Vicon Nexus Motion analysis, Figure 3). It is anticipated that the research undertaken will contribute to future ulcer prevention devices and/ or therapies.

Michael Hellen
PhD Student

Michael Hellen graduated from the University of Limerick in 2008with a BSc in Applied Physics. Throughout his four year degree he obtained industrial work experience and studied many fields such as electronics and medical physics and developed a keen interest and passion for medical device research and development grew.
In 2009, Michael graduated with an MSc in Biomedical Engineering which provided him with a broader knowledge base of the biomedical sector. As he always had a real fervor for research, he decided to pursue a PhD with MEDIC at the Cork Institute of Technology.

His research involves using 3D motion analysis to the assess motor and gait deficiencies in people with Spina Bifida. His research involves identifying the main muscle groups that contribute to the gait deficiencies caused by spina bifida. Pin pointing the muscle groups will allow them to be targeted in the production and development of assistive technology/devices.  Michael is motivated and inspired by making a difference and improving the quality of life of people with spina bifida as well as those who care for them.  By studying at CIT and working with top class engineers, researchers and staff at MEDIC, he can access both technical and clinical expertise as well as a state of the art gait laboratory. Michael’s vision is that at the end of his three year PhD program, the knowledge base for spina bifida gait disorder aetiology will have improved and that further work will lead to the development of new medical devices which have a direct and positive impact on the lives of the children with spina bifida and their families.

Shona Cunningham
PhD Student

Shona Cunningham graduated with a Bachelor of Engineering (Honours) in Mechanical Engineering from the Cork Institute of Technology (CIT) in 2009 and is one the three MEDIC PhD students. Shona is currently undertaking a project in the Smart Devices research strand of MEDIC, in collaboration with the National Marine College of Ireland (NMCI). Her PhD project focuses on scuba diving equipment known as a closed circuit rebreather (CCR) which allows deep sea divers to dive deeper with less equipment and for longer durations. This technology has become prevalent over recent years, but significant research is required to improve the technology. The CCR technology conserves expensive helium by recycling the breathing gas, by passing it through a carbon dioxide chemical scrubber and topping the resultant scrubbed gas up with pure oxygen to maintain the oxygen content.



The research challenge is to redesign the carbon dioxide scrubber unit (Fig.1) whilst developing a gas detection system monitoring carbon dioxide. The technology would allow more efficient decompression information and provide a safe means of monitoring and carbon dioxide. The Cork Institute of Technology and MEDIC, offer state of the art facilities needed for this project, as well as prototyping facilities for manufacturing parts and chemistry laboratories for access and testing of breathing simulation components.
