Medic to receive Silver Star today

Erik Ofgang

It was Oct. 12, 2010, and Navy medic Todd J. Angell, of Bethel, was on patrol with the Marines in the Helmand Province of Afghanistan when things went terribly wrong.

The patrol was clearing an enemy firing position in the village of Kunder. While returning to the trucks, a Marine traveling on foot stepped on an improvised explosive device. Several Marines were wounded and one lost a leg.

Angell -- who enlisted at age 17, was sent to war at 20, and is now 22 -- was about 500 yards away from the injured men and knew if he waited for the patrol's vehicles to navigate the difficult terrain, it would be too late for the Marine who lost his leg.

With no regard for his own safety, Angell ran to the injured Marine through an area known to contain explosives.

Angell said it was dark at the time, and he had no night gear because they had not planned for the patrol to last as long as it did.

"I just ran through the darkness of the night and basically followed where I thought I saw smoke," he said. "When I got close, I just started screaming, and my guys were screaming for me."

Angell tended to the most severely wounded Marine. He stopped the bleeding from the Marine's lost leg by adjusting a tourniquet and controlled the bleeding from his other wounds. Angell's efforts saved the Marine's life.

On Friday, Angell will be awarded a Silver Star for gallantry in action, the third highest U.S. military honor for combat valor, for his actions that day and on two other occasions. The medal will be presented at a ceremony at Camp Lejeune in North Carolina.

Angell said he enlisted in the Navy in 2007, so young that his parents had to sign a waiver. "I wanted to serve the country and to do emergency medicine in the military."

He said he decided to enlist after working as an emergency medical technician with the Bethel Fire Department and discovering that he performs well in high-pressure situations.

"The more hectic things are, the better focused I am. That's just the way I've always been," he said.

Afghanistan provided many opportunities for him to work in crisis situations. A petty officer third class, Angell was a corpsman with the 1st Section, 81st Platoon, Weapons Company, 1st Battalion, 8th Marines.

Angell's his Silver Star citation also mentions his lifesaving actions on Nov. 8 and Dec. 20, 2010.

On Nov. 8, Angell was on patrol when a member of the Afghanistan National Army struck an explosive device and lost both legs. Accompanied by a Marine, Angell tried to get to the injured soldier, but they hit a device together.

Though both were thrown into the air, they were relatively unscathed. "He was fine. I was a little dizzy, but I was OK," Angell said.

He continued through the explosive-ridden area to the wounded Afghan soldier, pulled him to safety and controlled his bleeding, which kept him alive until MedEvac helicopters arrived.

Later that day, he tended to an Afghan civilian who had been shot in the head and also saved his life.

"I worked on him for a little bit. I didn't think he was going to be able to make it, but he ended up making it. I saw him a month later and he was completely fine," Angell said.

On Dec. 20, Angell's patrol was caught in an extended firefight. Because the squad did not sustain any injuries, Angell put his combat training to good use.

He secured a doorway to a compound and accounted for two Taliban casualties, shooting accurately from a distance of 300 to 400 meters. He also helped man a 60-mm mortar gun.

Though it's not mentioned on his Silver Star citations, Angell also saved the life of his close friend Brandon Rumbaugh, who lost both his legs in November 2010. Rumbaugh will pin the medal on Angell in the ceremony Friday.

The only military honors higher than the Silver Star are the Distinguished Service Cross, Navy Cross, Air Force Cross and the Medal of Honor.

The award has to be given by a three-star general and three generals are expected to be present during Friday's ceremony.

Angell will be the grand marshal of Bethel's Memorial Day parade this year.

His older sisters, Kristen Angell, of Danbury, and Heather Angell, of Naugatuck, as well his mother, Mary Angell, and stepfather, Ed Krompinger, of Newtown, plan to attend the ceremony.

"It's such a prestigious honor. He's always been our hero, but he truly exemplified being a hero overseas and thinking about others before himself, and we're beyond proud," Kristen Angell said.

Their father, Ken Angell, died in 2008 and the family believes he was looking out for Todd in Afghanistan.

"It's funny how the world works. My father died before he could see my brother off to war. However, I believe he died so he could be his angel overseas, protect him and lead him out of harms way," Kristen said.

"There's really no reason (Todd) should be here after what he went through. He just as easily could have died, but there was an angel watching over him."

Todd Angell will leave the military next week. He hopes to pursue a career in law enforcement and has applied to a sheriff's office in Tampa, Fla.

"I'm trying to eventually get up to the sergeant level and continue serving, continue taking care of bad guys," he said. "The medical knowledge I have will always be there in the back of my head in case I ever need it."

As far as his lifesaving actions in Afghanistan, Angell said he grew close to the Marines he was stationed with and said they would have done anything they could to save each other's lives.

"These guys aren't just strangers to me, they're family. I would do it all again -- run through minefields or whatever. All that matters is there's somebody that needs help."
