Wars have changed combat medic training


By Michelle Tan - Staff writer
Posted : Sunday Sep 18, 2011 8:46:08 EDT

CAMP BULLIS, Texas — Exhausted and bloody, the soldiers staggered like zombies toward the battalion aid station at Forward Operating Base Courage.

They had head and chest wounds, missing limbs and gunshot wounds, and as they collapsed onto the dry, dusty ground, a swarm of medics rushed to their aid.

“I need a litter!”

“Get me some Kerlix [bandages]!”

The wounds weren’t real, but the scenario was as real as it could be for the medics — students in their final field training exercise before qualifying as combat medics — as they ran through a chaotic mass casualty incident at this training base just outside San Antonio.

By the numbers

• 68W: Combat medic military occupational specialty

• 18,300: Combat medics in the Army

• 7,500: Soldiers who become 68Ws each year

“The training is pretty challenging,” said Pfc. Nour Arodak, who will be assigned to the 101st Airborne Division after graduation. “We’re applying everything and we’re doing all the super hoo-ah stuff we’re supposed to know and we’re learning to work as a team. And it’s hotter than sin out here and you’re having to learn everything on exhaustion.”

The Army’s 68W combat medics are grown here in San Antonio; the 16-week course starts at Fort Sam Houston and culminates at Camp Bullis with a 12-day field training exercise.

More than 18,300-strong, combat medics make up the second-largest military occupational specialty in the Army, behind the infantry. And the demand for combat medics on the ground in Iraq and Afghanistan remains high.

“About 60 to 70 percent of students will deploy within six months of graduation from [advanced individual training],” said Capt. Jonathan Haines, commander of D Company, 232nd Medical Battalion, who recently had about 340 of his students at Camp Bullis for their field training exercise.

To date, 163 combat medics have died supporting operations Iraqi Freedom and Enduring Freedom.

To keep up with demand, the Army Medical Department Center and School produces about 7,500 68W soldiers a year, and instructors tasked with training these new medics constantly update their curriculum using lessons learned from the war zones.

What the Army has learned is that hemorrhage control is paramount, said Maj. Sharon Rosser, the officer in charge of the Soldier Medic Training Site, also known as FOB Courage.

Students must be able to apply an effective tourniquet in less than 60 seconds, she said.

They also focus on how to treat wounds to the inguinal, or groin, area, as well as wounds to the neck and axillary, or underarm, areas, Rosser said.

Those wounds have been common among troops in Afghanistan, and they are in areas of the body that aren’t easily tied off using a tourniquet, she said.

The Army also has changed the way it gives fluids to a patient, she said.

“When the war first started, we gave everyone fluids, which can add to hypothermia,” Rosser said. “We’ve moved to the theory of hypotensive resuscitation, where we give patients just enough fluid to keep a radial pulse.”

That means instead of infusing a patient with 3 liters of saline, combat medics now give their patients 500 milliliters of Hextend, a solution containing colloid properties that will nourish the body better than saline, she said.

Combat medics now also learn how to perform a surgical cricothyroidotomy, which also is known as an emergency airway puncture.

This procedure involves making an incision through a patient’s skin and cricothyroid membrane in the throat to create an airway and allow the patient to breathe.

Another lesson learned from Afghanistan that has found its way back to the schoolhouse is the use of trauma blankets on every patient being prepared for medical evacuation, Haines said.

The altitude and mountainous terrain in Afghanistan coupled with long helicopter flights from remote locations to the nearest hospital led to patients getting hypothermia without the blankets, he said.

Rosser and Haines both said they regularly seek feedback from combat medics in the field and from the Tactical Combat Casualty Care Committee, which evaluates lessons learned from theater and drives changes to the 68W training curriculum, for ways to update and improve the combat medic training.

“I take this training very personally,” said Haines, who has lost one of his own combat medics in the line of duty.

The training is designed to give the students confidence in their skills, Rosser said.

In addition to the mass casualty scenario at the battalion aid station, where the students learn how to triage and treat dozens of patients while managing limited resources and arranging for medical evacuation, they also run through convoy training and treat casualties while on a patrol.

To complete their field exercise, they must overcome the trauma lane portion of the training. Here, while under fire, they are forced to treat a casualty with one of the four life-threatening injuries most commonly faced by the troops, Rosser said.

“This is where they showcase what they learned in tactical field care,” said Rosser, who also is a physician assistant.

Students going through the trauma lane are timed and assessed, and they must treat a patient suffering either from an amputation and serious blood loss; an airway compromise that requires a surgical cricothyroidotomy; chest trauma requiring a needle decompression; or an inguinal, neck or axillary wound that isn’t easily treated with a tourniquet, Rosser said.

While most of the 68W medic training involves using students as mock casualties, the trauma lanes feature 180-pound mannequins loaded with about 30 pounds of equipment.

The trauma lane tests soon-to-be medics on their “bread and butter” skills, Haines said.

“We do try to create positive stress” during the training, he said. “It gives them what they might see when they deploy and teaches them to focus on what they’re doing and block out what’s going on around them. We want them to take what they learned from Day One and put it all together. A lot of this is a mental exercise.”

The training is critical because a medic could very well be the sole medical subject-matter expert in an infantry platoon, for example, Haines said.

Rosser agreed.

“We’re here to test them and make it realistic, not to trip them up,” she said.

Pfc. Thomas Wachter was all smiles after passing his trauma lane test. “I just validated, the last step in our training to see if we’re medics or not, if we’re capable of doing it,” he said.

Wachter’s trauma lane patient suffered from a gunshot wound to the neck and shrapnel in his right forearm.

The field training exercise has been invaluable, said Wachter, who will be going to airborne school and then to the 82nd Airborne Division after graduation.

“Instead of just talking about it [in the classroom], you’re actually doing it,” he said. “I’ve learned more in the last week and a half out here.”

Becoming a combat medic appealed to him because it still keeps him close to infantry soldiers but also gives him an additional skill, Wachter said.

“You’re still a grunt, but a specialized grunt,” he said.

Pfc. Justin Barbery, who said he originally wanted to be an infantryman, is headed to the 1st Armored Division after he graduates from AIT.

He said he studied and trained hard and is ready to deploy if called.

“For me, this is not just a job, but I’m someone who wants to bring soldiers back,” he said. “For me, it’s not how many soldiers I’m going to lose but how many I’m going to save, how many are going to come home to their families.”
