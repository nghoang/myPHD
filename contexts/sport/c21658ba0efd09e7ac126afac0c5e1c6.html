    After The Breeders' Cup, A Question Worth Asking: Is The Sport Of Horse Racing Really Dying?

    comments, called-out
    + Comment now
    + Comment now

    Classic winner, Fort Larned (Image credit: AFP/Getty Images via @daylife)

    Recently I wrote a story about Kevin Plank, the founder and chief executive of Under Armour, and his attemptÑthrough the purchase of the historic Sagamore FarmÑto reinvigorate the sport of thoroughbred horse racing in the state of Maryland. Many peopleÑfrom within the industry and withoutÑquestioned the sanity of PlankÕs purchase, mainly because, as I wrote: ÒThe sport of horse racing is perceived to be dying a slow, inexorable death in the U.S., an ember of the 20th-century sporting scene, like boxing.Ó

    Lately, IÕve been thinking about that line, wondering if, in fact, that perception is reality. Is horse racing really dying?

    The answer, I think, is both ÒyesÓ and Òno.Ó

    First, the dying part:

    There is no argument, even among the most strident fans of horse racing, that the sport is much, much less popular now than it was during its heyday (the 1930s through the 1950s), and even during the 1970s. Back then, it rivaled boxing and baseball as the sport in America.

    But now, when it comes to true relevance on the national sporting stage, itÕs basically been whittled down to the Triple Crown events and the BreedersÕ Cup. And it has become very dependent on star horses. Those eventsÑespecially the latter two Triple Crown races and the BreedersÕ CupÑget a big boost when there is either a Triple Crown threat (like 2012Õs IÕll Have Another, until he broke down before the Belmont) and Zenyatta, the first female horse to win the BreedersÕ Cup, in 2009. (That year, she was runner up to Serena Williams for the APÕs female athlete of the year award.)

    But the bigger problem might be on the grassroots level. Tracks have been in trouble all over the country and in Canada. There was talk recently of moving the Preakness from the state of Maryland. Hialeah in Florida closed down for eight years. The owner of CaliforniaÕs Santa Anita (Magna Entertainment) filed for bankruptcy a few years ago. And in the province of Ontario, the government has announced it will stop its slots revenue-sharing program, which could end up necessitating the close of two-thirds of the provinceÕs tracks.

    WageringÑthe lifeblood of the sportÑis down 30% over the last decade in the U.S., and thereÕs been a 10% drop, year-to-year, since 2008 (though perhaps things have stabilized a bit: wagering is up 2% this year).

    The Kentucky Derby, at least in terms of television, is the sportÕs only relevant event. The list of the top 50 most-viewed sporting events of 2012 from the first half of the year (in other words, before the Olympics) includes healthy helpings of football, basketball, golf and NASCAR. One horse race made the list: The Derby, of course.

    The sport also took a huge reputational hit this year, as well, with the extensive New York Times series that painted horse racing as a callous industry rife with drugs, gambling and dead horses.

    In the end, maybe there was no one thing that causes the sportÕs decline; it was the accumulation of a thousand small-to-medium-sized cuts.

    **

    But there is another side to the story.

    I recently had a chat with Craig Fravel, who has been the chief executive and president of the BreedersÕ Cup since July 2011. Fravel, a lawyer by trade, has been in the horse racing business for 21 years. He recently negotiated a deal to move the BreedersÕ Cup, which was run last weekend, from ESPN/ABC to NBC, its home network from 1985-2004 (the terms were not disclosed). Outside of the Triple Crown events, the BreedersÕ Cup is the biggest thing going in the sport, with 15 races over the two-day period, and total prize money of $25 million, the largest amount in the U.S., and one of the biggest purses in world sport. The Classic is the main race, with a purse of $5 million.

    Fravel, as might be expected, goes against the conventional wisdom when it comes to his sport. ÒI firmly believe this is not a dying industry, and IÕve been hearing that since 1990,Ó he says. ÒI feel like itÕs like a lot of other industries. Those who do it well are successful. Those who donÕt, arenÕt.Ó

    Fravel concedes that the financial collapse of 2008 was a harsh blow to horse racing which, from a horse-buying perspective, is a luxury. ÒNo one needs a race horse, not like you need a car or a house,Ó says Fravel. Wealthy buyers, particularly in 2009 and 2010, says Fravel, were reluctant when it came to buying horses, worried about the countryÕs economic future.

    But that began to change this year. At the three major yearling sales, sales were up significantly. At the Barretts sale this month, 174 yearlings were purchased for $4 million, a 54% jump in sales. At Keeneland, sales were up 22%. And at the Fasig-Tipton sale at Saratogo, the average horse price rose 16%. The bulk of the Saratoga money came from Sheikh Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum, the prime minister of UAE, who spent $8.5 million on 13 horses.

    These sale numbers are particularly important to the BreedersÕ Cup. ÒThe breeding and sale of horses is our fundamental mission at the end of the day,Ó says Fravel. ÒItÕs the reason we exist.Ó

    Kevin Plank, part of the new generation of owners. (Image credit: Getty Images via @daylife)

    The big spenders, like the Sheikh and B. Wayne Hughes (the founder of Public Storage) and Benjamin Leon, Jr., certainly help the sport. But perhaps more promising for the industry is the growing Òmiddle classÓ stratum of buyers, like Kevin Plank and vitaminwater founder, Mike Repole, who are taking a slow, steady and smart approach to building their horse-racing entities.

    And TV ratings for this yearÕs Cup Classic, won by 9-1 long shot, Fort Larned? They improved to a 2.2 Nielsen share, far behind the weekendÕs college football games (Alabama-LSU; Oregon-USC), but better than last yearÕs 1.2 share.

    **

    ÒThe sport is never going to be what it was in 1958,Ó says Bill Finley, the fine racing writer for ESPN. ÒBut the doom and gloom has gone way overboard. The sport has bottomed out and found its new place in this over-populated gambling world. ItÕs most likely not going anywhere.Ó

    To this ink-stained wretch, the sport of horse racing has a familiar feel. It looks to me a bit like print journalism. ItÕs been diminished, as new, flashier things have come along. ItÕs been diminished, most likely for good. But it will survive.

    Horse racing is dead. Long live horse racing.
