Baseball the Early Years
By John Little

Few would argue that baseball has become America's national sport, especially from spring time through to the World Series October climax.
Although the exact origin of baseball is still in doubt, the first time it was mentioned in print was in the English author, John Newbery's 1744 tome, A Little Pretty Pocket-Book. Less than half a century later the game leapt from the pages to the field of play. In 1791 a decree banning the playing of the game within eight yards of the town hall was passed. Thanks to the contribution of Alexander Joy Cartwright (1820-1892), the game developed structure. An early New Yorker, he went on to invent the modern baseball field in 1845. Cartwright and the members of his New York Knickerbockers Base Ball Club, devised the first rules and regulations for the modern game of baseball.

England Thrash Minnows as Rivals Held by Moldova World Cup Qualifier ? Brasil 2014

From the News Desk: October 15, 2012
by Peter Law
Print    Send to a Friend

The Stall is Set Out
The third match of England’s qualifying campaign for Brazil 2014 saw the visit of San Marino – one of Europe’s also-ran s- to Wembley. Without a single victory in a competitive international, the visitors arrived, set on blanket defence and dogged defending. Roy Hodgson’s side were expected to run in the goals ahead of a more demanding trip to Poland next week.

Without the suspended Steven Gerrard and Glen Johnson, Wayne Rooney was appointed skipper as England looked to boost their goal difference after dropping points in the 1-1 home draw against Ukraine. The Three Lions took control of the game right from the start, with the visitors lining up with a five man backline augmented by four deep-lying midfielders. The game settled into an unchanging pattern, England laying siege to the San Marino goal with virtually all the play taking place in the visitors half.

However, with space at a premium England struggled to find a breakthrough. The loss of Walcott – brought down by keeper Aldo Simoncini – saw the introduction of Aaron Lennon as a substitute, but all too often England tried to play through the middle.

Inevitable
A deep defence were able to hold out for 35 minutes as England’s attacking play lacked urgency and fluency, as too often an overworked defence were able to get a foot in and frustrate the hosts. Enjoying almost 80% of possession, and forcing corners freely, Hodgson’s side were often too predictable while continuing to spurn a few good chances.

On the half hour Michael Carrick saw a powerful effort come back off the crossbar, with Danny Welbeck striking a post off the rebound. “I thought we would win,” Hodgson said on the play up to that point. With pressure telling on San Marino, which boasts a population of just 30,000, it was no surprise when Welbeck earned a penalty that Rooney despatched to give England  a 35th minute lead. Then just two minutes later, Welbeck made it 2-0 with a neat finish at the near post after a Lennon cross.

Two up at the interval, the game was virtually won but rather than confirm their superiority, England came out rather flat, and despite making chances constantly took time to move further ahead. Once or twice the opposition got a few men into the England half, and on the hour lone striker Ezequiel Rinaldi Danilo ran past Gary Cahill to fire their only chance wide.

Focus
When the goals came, just like the first half they came in an eight minute spell. First skipper Rooney fired home a right footed effort to notch his 31st international goal after sixty-nine minutes. Then two minutes later Tom Cleverley set up Welbeck for the striker to score his second goal. Young Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain clipped home England’s fifth goal after 77 minutes before sub Andy Carroll saw a couple of late chances forcing some good saves.

With Ukraine held to a scoreless draw in Moldova, England remains top of their group. However, against San Marino they spurned plenty of chances in a game where they forced 17 corners without reply and amassed over 30 attempts on goal. Attention moves to Warsaw on Tuesday next, where the game is certain to be a more testing affair.


Levante Tear up Form Book in Derby

From the News Desk: October 7, 2012
by satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

Quality
Levante coach Juan Ignacio Martínez celebrated a perfect end to a horrible week with a win 1-0 win over city rivals València thanks to a superb strike by new Nigerian signing Obafemi Martins. “Martins is important to our team,” he said. “All of our players are. It was an important goal.”

Martins expressed delight. “My goals are important to me, but three points matter more,” he said. A frantic derby with committed challenges from both sides was decided by a route one pass out of defence by goalkeeper Gustavo Adolfo Munúa after João Pereira’s pass had split Levante’s defence and forced Munúa to thwart Andrés Guardado’s effort.

Munúa launched the ball up the field and it was latched onto by Martins who lashed an unstoppable half-volley to Vicente Guaita’s left, giving Mauricio Pellegrino’s first choice keeper had no chance. As goals go it was a special effort worthy of deciding any match, even a derby of this importance. Next weekend sees the international break – one that will decide whether Martínez will have to do without Martins for up five weeks in the New Year.

Early Exchanges
There were rustic challenges without being a dirty match, arguments, passion, bookings, but no red cards and chances galore, but a lack of cutting edge finishing – a strange thing to say with los Che’s captain Roberto Soldado on the pitch.

His understanding with Brasilian Jonas Gonçalves is blossoming as València had the best of the early chances. Soldado miss-control cost him an easy chance, but his interplay with Jonas on the edge of the area put his partner through only to be denied by Munúa’s legs at the expense of a corner. Guardado’s cross ought to have been dispatched by Soldado, but he headed over the bar.

Victor Ruiz and Tino Costa earned yellow cards for bad fouls on Martins and José Barkero in the final 15 minutes of the first half, but sandwiched in between was excellent refereeing by Carlos del Cerro Grande, who booked Levante’s captain Sergio Ballesteros for feigning injury to get an opponent booked.

Jonas earned the displeasure of both the referee and Levante’s players for a committed challenge that left Munúa in a heap as the keeper bravely dived at the striker’s feet. Jonas had every right to challenge and the booking seemed harsh – there was no intent. Nevertheless, he apologised to the keeper.

Fernando Gago had clearly forgotten his shooting boots. Two long range shots in the second half easily cleared the bar – they would have missed a goal on top. But clear chances were difficult for València to create, although Aly Cissokho’s cross for Soldado at least resulted in a header that made Munúa work. As València pressed for an equaliser, they became more and more prone to the counter attack.

Former Liverpool player Nabil el Zhar had a couple of chances that – one of which required an excellent parry by Guaita. The other was blocked. His replacement Pedro Ríos had the best opportunity to settle the match as injury time approached. A four against one attack deserved a better end than a Guaita save from Ríos’ shot, but Levante won’t complain. They held on to secure a famous win.

An Important Break
Samson Siasia was replaced by Stephen Keshi as Super-Eagles boss after Nigeria’s shock failure to qualify for the African Cup of Nations last year. The Confédération Africaine de Football (CAF) decided to change the competition to odd years in order to avoid tiredness in World Cup years. That means another tournament will take place in 2013. Libya has bee replaced as hosts by South Africa.

Among the casualties of the new qualification system is Egypt – losers to the Central African Republic in Round 2. Nigeria made it to the final round of qualifiers. October’s break will decide if they and the recalled Martins will go to South Africa. Keshi will no doubt have noted Martins’ performance this afternoon.

Unlike many European coaches Martínez respects the African Cup of Nations and has no problem with it. “It is a prestigious competition,” he said, “but I am focused on València.” He knows that he could lose his top striker for up to five weeks, but Levante knew that when they signed Martins. His goal this afternoon has already paid off a large slice of their investment even if they lose him for up to five weeks next year.


An Historic Rivalry

From the News Desk: October 7, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

 Proud Tradition
Levante Unión Deportiva has a proud history of resistance and social justice, drawing its support from the dockers and working class – its origins were in the docks of València. The city itself has a proud history too. València was the capital of the legendary thirteenth century warrior king Jaume I el Conqueridor, who drove the Muslim rulers out of both València and nearby cities too.

The monastery of El Puig is testament to Jaume’s prowess. It’s capture signalled the end of Islamic rule over the region and resulted in Jaume’s domain reaching the kingdom of his father-in-law, another great, but much different Spanish monarch Alfonso X el Sabio (the Wise), whose approach was far more enlightened and eventually resulted in his overthrow through plotting by the Pope and Alfonso’s courtiers in 1282.

Valèncian resistance continued into the twentieth century through the ill-fated government of author Vicente Blasco Ibañez and its role in Republican Spain. It rather than Barçelona was actually the capital of the Republic. València was the last city to surrender to Franco and paid for it with years of neglect and worse, especially.

Republican Football
Founded ten years before its more famous rival Levante holds the distinction of being the last holders of the Copa España Libre (the Cup of Free Spain), beating València 1-0 in the final in 1937 in Español’s Montjuic Stadium. The Spanish Civil War and the dictator Francisco Franco hatred of that competition ensured that Levante hold that title in perpetuity.

València at least competed, losing to their city rivals. Barçelona opted to tour México and the USA instead.

Franco punished football in both Barçelona and València too, brazenly favouring Real Madrid – a source of extra spice in the Classico and one that established Valèncian clubs as rivals too, but the historic ties establish Valèncian clubs as rivals of Barçelona too. Despite Jaume’s conquest of the city – a task that took 16 years – València never saw itself as part of Cataluña. It still doesn’t.

The City Rivalry
Levante supporters see themselves as working class and left wing and there is some resentment of their more successful city rivals, which they see as right-wing and favoured at their expense. València certainly has right-wing fans – the ultras Yomus for example, but Gol Gran never shared the racist or political outlook of Yomus.

The club managed to unite the peñas into one support base at Mestalla to provide unified support for the team. It’s needed as the financial crisis has affected attendances at Mestalla.
Both coaches Levante’s Juan Ignacio Martínez and València’s Mauricio Pellegrino acknowledged the importance of the fixture, but also of fair play too, but this is a derby and it is at Levante’s Estadi Ciutat de València. Anything can happen.

Reversal of Fortune
A week ago the form books told a different story. València was hovering in the wrong half of the table with just one win in La Liga and Europe too. Levante, the oldest club in the region, founded 103 years ago was perched two pints above their city rivals, but the next two matches for both teams reversed both form and confidence.

Mauricio Pellegrino’s new-look Valencia team dispatched last season’s miracle team – Manolo Jiménez had performed heroics in the historic city of Saragossa, defying the odds to enable Real Zaragoza to somehow avoid relegation. Valencia won 2-0 last Saturday despite the harsh sending off of Algerian Sofiane Feghouli with the best part of half an hour remaining. LOSC Lille proved no match at Mestalla on Tuesday either, losing by the same score.

Meanwhile, Levante suffered the reverse. Bottom-placed Osasuna thrashed Martínez’ side 4-0 in Pamplona. “The results were horrible last week,” he said, “but I am focused on València tomorrow.” It got worse as despite taking the lead against Hannover 96 in Germany in the Europa League and having a man advantage for 80 minutes, Levante surrendered the advantage to lose 2-1 on Thursday, meaning they are less rested and shorn of confidence.

A Special Challenge
València’s French left-back Aly Cissokho explained his hopes. “For me València is a big team with a big stadium and I want to discover a new challenge,” he told us. “After [Real] Madrid and Barçelona, València is the best. I hope to give the best for this challenge.”

He is looking forward to his first Derby of València. “When I come to the centre of town everyone is speaking about this confrontation,” Cissokho told us. “Everybody wants to win, but I think if we play our game, we will win this game, but it will be difficult because it’s very difficult to play a derby, but we have to rest and play our game against Levante.”


The Rise and Fall and Rise Again of Levante

From the News Desk: October 6, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

Importance
It isn’t the Classico which will also happen tomorrow, but València’s derby has no shortage of passion and incident. The doom and gloomers were forced to eat their words when the predicted failure did not occur. Instead, Levante surprised many last season with a fantastic campaign that briefly saw them emerge as the main challengers to Real Madrid and Barçelona, even topping the table in October. They were overtaken by València and also Málaga for Champion’s League spots too.

The latter secured the final Champion’s League spot by just one point from Atlético de Madrid and Levante. The latter didn’t have the resources thrown at them by Málaga’s filthy rich owners, or the services of the prolific Colombian striker Radamel Falcao.

Abdullah bin Nasser bin Abdullah Al Ahmed Al Thani is one of the richest people in the world and owner of Málaga as well as being related to the ruler of Qatar. Levante could not compete with that financial clout and nor in fact could València or others too. Given their previous experience in La Liga Levante’s rise from the ashes is nothing short of phenomenal.

From Rack and Ruin to the Top Again
Their previous foray into the exalted territory of the Primera División began promisingly a 4-2 win over València at their Estadi Ciutat de València in the 2006-7 season being the highlight, but it couldn’t last. A year later an ignominious return to the second division occurred. Players had not been paid for several weeks and morale and form plumbed new depths.

In fact it emerged that they were owed €18m. Protests occurred and a strike was threatened. They went into free-fall and their exit from La Liga was confirmed several matches before the end of the season. An extra match was even arranged for the end of the season, where La Liga players united to play a benefit match against Levante to pay their players.

It remains a dark period in the club’s history – one that forced a change in approach and a fiscally responsible approach. Levante’s exile was short-lived. After two seasons in Segunda División A, the Estadi Ciutat de València would witness top flight football once more.

Luis García Plaza took over coaching Levante in 2008. He left last year after taking Levante back to La Liga to coach Getafe, but his former charges outplayed his new team to secure European football for the first time in the club’s history.

A large part of the credit for Levante’s La Liga resurgence must go to current coach Juan Ignacio Martínez. A run of seven consecutive wins a year ago saw Levante sit proudly at the top of the Primera División. It would take a brave or foolhardy person to write off those achievements even though by his own admission ‘it’s been a horrible week’.

The Form Book
It started with Levante in form in the right half of the table and tomorrows opponents València in the wrong half. Last weekend bottom of the table Osasuna thrashed Martínez’ side 4-0 in Pamplona and despite taking an early lead against Hannover 96 on Thursday and having a man advantage for the best part of 80 minutes, the Germans still took the 3 points. Despite the loss Levante is in second place in Group L of the Europa League having beaten Sweden’s Helsingborg a fortnight earlier.

Meanwhile, Mauricio Pellegrino succeeded Russia-bound Unai Emery at Mestalla and reversed a bad start last week with a brace of 2-0 wins against Real Zaragoza and LOSC Lille, although despite securing second place in their Champion’s League Group, they face a tricky trip to Belarus to face Bayern Munich’s conquerors BATE Borisov next. However both Pellegrino and Martínez had only one thing on their minds at this afternoon’s press conference – tomorrow’s derby of València.


The Dark Arts

From the News Desk: October 6, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

Stops and Starts
Manchester United’s manager Sir Alex Fergusson recently called referee Chris Foy’s decision to allow 4 minutes injury time in a match against Spurs ‘a disgrace.’ It is unusual to allow that long but although some claim Ferguson gets an almost special dispensation called Fergie-time occasionally, but whatever the rights and wrongs of Ferguson’s complaints in that match it ignores a far worse scandal – time-wasting is rife and totally within the rules.
The game is studded with stops and starts already. Preventing the taking of free-kicks and throw-ins quickly, taking their sweet time over goal-kicks, can be penalised by officials, but how often does that occur?

And then there are the deliberate time-wasting manoeuvres that are perfectly legal. What are tactical substitutions in the last few minutes or even in injury time for if not a cynical attempt to waste time? Rarely referees lose patience and punish it. Manuel Rui Costa was once sent off for taking too long to depart when substituted. Don’t these dubious methods amount to cheating and affect the flow of the game far more than using technology?

And what about taking the ball to the corner flag and setting up camp there using their body to prevent opponents being able to challenge them? Isn’t that obstruction and ungentlemanly conduct, but is that complained about or acted against? How does that not interrupt the flow of the game? Referees don’t add time for teams doing that. Let’s face facts; the beautiful game can be made pretty ugly within the rules. Wasting time while keeping the ball in play is a time honoured tactic and entirely within the rules.

Double Standards
Hardly anyone comments on this. Supporters pay for 90 minutes of football. Let’s be honest; they are not getting what they paid for and not because Foy didn’t allow a little more injury time in one match. They never get the full 90 minutes in any match – never close to it. But who complains that fans are being short-changed?

There are plenty of complaints that players are tired the longer a season progresses, but none about the fans – without whom there is no sport – not getting what they pay for. No managers or administrators or even sports journalists take up cudgels on behalf of  the short-changed fans, but when using technology is mentioned, it’s hard to find any of them without an opinion – usually that it will ruin the flow of the game.

Is stopping the game for a few seconds to make sure that the right decision is reached really such a bad thing, especially when the rewards are now so high and the consequences of failure so dire? Other sports use technology and get the decisions right. It has hardly brought the foundations of those sports crashing down. Why should football alone be stuck in a myopic past, immune from the benefits of progress?


Myopic

From the News Desk: October 6, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

Rewards and Punishments
With UEFA’s policy of Financial Fair Play about to bite deeply, the consequences of poor decisions or even honest mistakes have never been higher. An injustice here or there can cost a goal or gift an undeserved one that affects a result. The claim that it will even out over a season is scant consolation to a manager sacked when confidence is lost and not winning becomes a habit.

Sometimes they don’t have consequences, or appear not to. The recent match between Valencia and LOSC Lille ended in 2-0 win for Valencia, but the referee Gianluca Rocchi’s performance was inconsistent on occasion and bizarre on others. Valencia’s new left-back Aly Cissokho refused to criticise Rocchi. “The referee for me tonight was more or less [okay],” Cissokho said. “It’s football, but tonight we win, so we don’t speak about the referee.”

But what if a bad decision had cost a goal and that goal was the difference between reaching the knock-out stage of the Champion’s League, or even the safety net of the latter stage of the Europa League? That affects finances and confidence and the ability to meet targets and the jobs of coaches in particular. It even affects the coefficients that determine European places for the various leagues.

Luck of the Draw
“This season, of course the target is the same as last years; finish La Liga in Champion’s League position,” Valencia’s Director of Communications Damia Vidagany explains for us. “It’s necessary for the club; it’s our main goal and then like it happened last season, we play a semi-final in La Copa and semi-final in Europa League – fight for the competitions.”

Vidagany accepts that there will be some external circumstances beyond anyone’s control. “We’ll be sure that if the draws are not unlucky for us, as you know to play with Real Madrid and Barcelona in the first rounds is very difficult for every team,” he says. “Maybe we are going to play, or we can play a final, or maybe the goal is to finish in La Liga in the third position and then to play La Copa del Rey the final.”

Errors of Judgement
The draw to any competition is outside his control, or anyone’s for that matter, but mistakes, especially correctable ones, are a different matter. The recent Europa League match between Tottenham Hotspur and Lazio at White Hart Lane illustrates this as Spurs had two goals disallowed that technology showed should have counted.

That not only cost Spurs two points, but gifted Lazio one and gave an advantage to the two other teams in the group, especially Maribor. Doubtless the officials reached their decisions honestly, but they were wrong and have consequences – decisions that technology could have put right easily.

The second round resulted in Spurs drawing against Panathinaikos and Lazio beating Maribor, leaving Spurs in third place. Had the goals been given and other results remained as they were André Villas-Boas’ team would be top to the group now rather than third with difficult matches to come. And if Spurs miss out on the knockout stage by a point, will UEFA compensate Daniel Levy or Villas-Boas if it costs him his job for missing out on the greater riches they would have had.

Unlike his predecessor at White Hart Lane, Harry Redknapp, who viewed the Europa League as a punishment – wasn’t alone in that view in England – Villas-Boas takes it seriously. And if poor decisions cost a team qualification for the Champion’s League and a manager, especially Villas-Boas, his job will the league’s authorities compensate the manager for losing his job and the damage to his reputation too?


Technology controversy reignited as Spurs are left thwarted by poor decisions

From the News Desk: October 6, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

An Impossible Task
Football has proved unduly resistant to using technology. It is often claimed by so-called purists that it would stop the flow of the game. Really? Perhaps they should check the statistics on how long the ball is actually in play during matches before making such claims. They’d be surprised if they don’t already know and choose to ignore inconvenient facts that the game doesn’t flow already.

I’ll be honest. The first time I saw Clint Dempsey’s recent goal against Lazio in actual time I thought he was clearly offside. I was wrong, but it took a replay frozen at the precise moment that the cross was delivered to prove it. That goal should have stood, but no official could be faulted for getting that decision wrong – it was impossible to be certain at actual speed as it involved a few centimetres and with players running, rather than the still frame that resolved it conclusively.

The officials did not have the benefit of seeing that replay. I have lost count of the number of managers or players who complain when a decision goes against them, sometimes with justification, like Dempsey’s, but what about solutions? The obvious one is using technology. Football has the resources to get it right and in this case a simple replay proved the injustice beyond doubt, but opposition to using technology in the game is rife.

Perspectives
Lazio’s coach Vladimir Petkovi?’s opinion was perhaps predictable. “I’d have to see the incidents myself back on tape,” he said, “but I could say myself that there were lots of things that went for us or went against us. Fouls perhaps committed against us, but it’s the sort of thing you have to look at after the game, so no other comment on that.”

Villas-Boas had been on the receiving end of the wrong decisions, so what does he think? “I seek an explanation,” he said. “I don’t think it’s a foul. It’s the ref’s [Ovidu Alinhategan] decision. It’s done and it’s gone by.”

But Villas-Boas clearly felt aggrieved. “We don’t see nothing, so the feeling is exactly that we don’t see nothing from Caulker to prevent him from you know to prevent a foul that has been given,” The Europa-Cup-winning coach said. “I think you know that [Stefano] Mauri is one of the strongest players in the air with Lazio and certainly Caulker beats him in the air and scores a legal goal, so we don’t see nothing [wrong]. The disappointment comes from exactly that situation and obviously [Clint] Dempsey’s goal.”

Solutions
So what should be done about it, especially as the Caulker decision came from a goal-line official as Alinhategan’s view was blocked by Dempsey? “Unfortunately the ref ruled out the goals that perhaps we deserved to score, but it was a great game,” Villas-Boas said. “Well I think the technology in the offside is extremely difficult and I think it will slow down all of the running of the ninety minutes of the game.”

The Target

From the News Desk: October 2, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

Immediate Business
Fresh from an impressive 2-0 win over Real Zaragoza on Saturday, new coach Mauricio Pellegrino had no doubt what the priority was. “Tuesday,” he said. “My ambitions are limited to preparing for Tuesday now,” he said. Valencia entertain Lille tonight after losing to last year’s beaten finalists Bayern München a fortnight ago.

Pellegrino is not alone in recognising that Valencia has failed to deliver in Europe’s top competition since the glory days of 2001 and 2002 when los Che reached the final twice consecutively, losing to Bayern München and Real Madrid. But while Valencia failed to claim the Champion’s League title, former coach Rafa Benítez delivered a historic double in 2004 La Liga and the UEFA Cup.

Benítez walked out afterwards and delivered the major prize for his new club the following season. Meanwhile, the landscape in the historic city that was once the capital of the warrior monarch Jaume I el Conquistador changed. The writing on the wall remained unread until crisis loomed.

Shorn of resources to compete for the best players in the world, La Liga’s test of competition faded to a distant third as they tried – they trailed second place Barcelona by 30 points last season and La Liga Champions Real Madrid by almost 40. It was a bitter pill for supporters to swallow.

“Every man has dreams and our dream is maybe one day to win the league, but we must be realistic and at this moment Real Madrid and Barcelona – both teams are the best in the world – but we show in the first match in La Liga in Madrid, we draw 1-1 against Real Madrid, we are ready to fight,” says Valencia’s Director of Communications Damia Vidagany exclusively to Empower-Sport. “I don’t know if our power will be enough to fight for the league, but we are sure that we will be closer than last year’s.”

Targets
Unai Emery, recruited from Almeria after the disastrous few months in charge of Ronald Koeman failed to deliver. Two semi-finals of the new Europa League and a Copa del Rey semi-final last season were the highlights of Emery’s reign in Valencia. Pellegrino knows that he has chalice with a bitter taste, if not poisoned.

Last year los Che was dumped out of the Champion’s League with a poor showing against eventual winners Chelsea at Stamford Bridge. I mean it will be very difficult.”

An opening day defeat in Germany leaves Pellegrino’s team in need of points, hence his focus on tonight’s match.

Step By Step
“The most important thing is not to over-pressure the team,” Vidagany explains. “We know that we have a good team, but we are not thinking now in the pressure that we have to win in Europe especially.”

Vidagany maintains a realistic approach to Valencia’s ambitions. “You know that there is a lot of teams with a lot of money, more than Valencia could afford,” he says. “For example, Real Madrid and they are waiting for I think it’s almost ten years to win the title. It’s not good, the pressure.”

He knows that the team and its capabilities have changed, but he is determined to be part of a new era in Valencia that ultimately delivers more than the halcyon days managed to. “We have to work step by step,” says Vidagany. “We are sure that we are going to have our chance in the next three or four years and then when we are installed in the new stadium, maybe will be the moment to jump and pressure the team to win titles.”


Social Justice and Football

From the News Desk: July 31, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

A Long Road
Politics and sport may not mix, but football, the most popular sport in the world has social responsibilities. For many years it was used and abused by vile dictators including Benito Mussolini and Generalissimo Francisco Franco. It distracted people from the horrors of brutal regimes – a theme we have reported on previously.

The sport’s governing bodies were slow to respond – perhaps they could not. Football developed patriotism that was exploited by unscrupulous rulers, but the sport and its governing bodies deserve too.  The Confédération Africaine de Football (CAF) threw South Africa – then struggling under the inhuman yoke of apartheid out of the first African Cup of Nations in 1957 because the racists refused to send a team where race did not matter.

FIFA recognised the Makana FA of Robben Island. It is still respected in that country for its stance against apartheid when other sports – notably rugby and cricket dithered.

CAF also donated a significant amount to the African Union Commission to combat wars in the continent and praised Mali’s Seydou Keita for his emotional appeal for peace in his country – a call we covered at the time. In previous tournaments Keita could have expected a fine for his comments. This time CAF’s Head of Communications ensured we got a second chance to talk to him about such important comments.

Better Late Than Never
The message of safety and fighting HIV/AIDS is at last being taken seriously by football’s governing bodies. Empower-Sport attended the recent African Cup of Nations and Euro 2012. We are pleased to report that both events took a strong stand against HIV/AIDS.
demonstrated both its social commitment and determination to use the unifying power of football as the motor for social change beginning in January in Gabon.

CAF President Issa Hayatou, First Lady of Gabon, Sylvia Bongo Ondimba and Samuel Eto’o launched 3-0 a major drive against HIV and AIDS. It aimed to persuade young Africans to get tested and provided the facilities to do so. Eto’o was the highest profile player to lend his name to the campaign. But one of Gabon’s finest ever players was proud to assist, Pierre Aubameyang [his son Pierre-Emerick currently plays in France and for Gabon]. Aubameyang Snr, a talented midfielder in his own right, paved the way for other Gabonese to pay in Europe in the 80s. He currently coaches Gabon’s second team and FC Sapins, but is also an active supporter of the First Lady’s Foundation.

“When talking about football we forget about everything for 90 minutes and people forget about their real problems,” Aubameyang said. “She [Sylvia] had the lucidity to talk about some really important points. I really support her and I think that big events should come with these kind of initiatives.”

Aubameyang believes that football can and must tackle this scourge in Africa. “AIDS has been killing African people for years now and there are a lot of deaths per year,” he says. “I am actually an honourary member of the Sylvia Bongo Ondimba Foundation and I help her and am willing to work on this. I think it is good initiative to put the African Cup of Nations together with campaigns against AIDS, poverty and violence.”

An Unwitting Own-Goal
UEFA followed CAF’s lead by supporting an Anti-AIDS concert in Kiev’s Independence Square, which was full to capacity. It was organised by the Elena Pinchuk Foundation (see Exceptional, which was published previously). The aim was to raise awareness of the problem of HIV/AIDS in Ukraine. It succeeded greatly. I attended on behalf of Empower-Sport.

A number of false views of Ukraine were destroyed that night. Ukraine cares and is full of friendly people. There was not the slightest concern at the concert – no crimes reported or committed. It was packed with mainly young people partying for a very good cause. Sir Elton John performed first. He loves Ukraine and the feeling is mutual. His set seemed hard to follow, but Queen managed it – both performing for over an hour. The concert went on until well past midnight – there were no complaints.

Former England international warned non-white supporters not to go to Ukraine or Poland on Panorama’s outrageous Stadiums of Hate. I went and did not regret it. Ukrainians and Poles are not inherently racist – they are the opposite. There are racist incidents in those countries, just as there are in England.

At close to 3.00 a.m. walking around Kyiv at night, the only Ukrainians I encountered wanted to help and celebrate. I was invited to go drinking with Ukrainian supporters I came across at their expense. I didn’t fear for my life in Kyiv, or in Poland for that matter. I had no reason to.


The only negative note was that UEFA failed to send delegates to the concert on the eve of the final. It deserved the attendance of UEFA President Michel Platini and it should have been mentioned in his press conference earlier that day. Nevertheless, UEFA supporting such initiatives is definitely a welcome development and hopefully there will be many more.

From the News Desk: September 20, 2012
by Satish Sekar
Print    Send to a Friend

An Unusual Hat-trick
Andre Villas-Boas’ Tottenham Hotspur side were held to a goalless draw by Lazio at White Hart Lane tonight thanks in part to officiating errors. Spurs were wrongly denied two goals that technology – simple replays – could have resolved easily. Deadline-day signing US international Clint Dempsey seemed offside at first glance, but replays proved that he was marginally onside when he headed past Lazio keeper Federico Marchetti from 6 yards out.


Welshman Gareth Bale started on Spurs’ left wing. Bale’s cross was converted by Dempsey with Marchetti rooted to the spot after 22 minutes. With UEFA’s President Michel Platini , an ardent opponent of technology in football, watching, the erroneous decision, albeit a marginal one, looks set to ignite the technology debate once more, especially as Spurs were denied another goal almost 50 minutes later, when Stephen Caulker’s header was chalked off for impeding Stefano Mauri.

The referee’s view was blocked by Dempsey, suggesting that the goal-line official must have given it. Replays showed that it was a poor decision. Bale also had an effort disallowed, but the whistle had gone before his shot from outside the area was disallowed by the Romanian referee, Ovidu Alinhategan just before the hour mark.


Performance
Nevertheless, Villas-Boas was satisfied with the result given the circumstances. “I think first we are very happy with the performance,” he said. “I mean we played very well and I’m extremely happy for the effort that we put in, you know, in a competition where we are trying to change the way that we look at it in this country, as difficult as it looks, but extremely happy.” It seemed to be said through clenched teeth. Nevertheless, he praised the effort his players put in.


“The players put a tremendous effort in, looked positive and attacking throughout the whole of the game and managed to put the ball in the back of the net three times, but because of some mistakes from the ref, we couldn’t get the goals that we certainly deserved this evening,” Villas-Boas explained. “I mean in the end obviously we take the point against the team that has been performing well in Italy and look forward for these two away games, where we try to get the first win to get us on route to qualification.”

Lazio coach Vladimir Petkovi? disagreed, believing that his team fully deserved the draw. “I think at times we did find ourselves under pressure, but we coped well with that,” he said. “We also played in such a way that we created pressure and put pressure upon Tottenham as well and we created a couple of good chances, but I think ultimately you have to say that 0-0 was a good result for us and that we deserved that result.”


Diverse Opinions
Villas-Boas accepted Alinhategan’s decisions, but had little doubt that Spurs had been deprived of two goals that should have stood. “I don’t think it’s a foul,” the former Porto and Chelsea coach said. “It’s the ref’s decision. It’s done and it’s gone by.”

However, Villas-Boas was adamant that the goals should have stood. “We don’t see nothing, so the feeling is exactly that we don’t see nothing from Caulker to prevent him from, you know, to prevent a foul that has been given,” said Villas-Boas. “I think you know that Mauri is one of the strongest players in the air with Lazio and certainly Caulker beats him in the air and scores a legal goal, so we don’t see nothing. The disappointment comes from exactly that situation and obviously Dempsey’s goal.”

Villas-Boas supports the use of some technology even though UEFA President Michel Platini, who attended the match, is an implacable opponent of technology. Meanwhile, Petkovi? offered a totally different perspective.

“Well I’d have to see the incidents myself back on tape,” he said, “but I could say myself that there were lots of things that went for us or went against us, fouls perhaps committed against us, but it’s the sort of thing you have to look at after the game, so no other comment on that.”
