    The Sport of Politics: May the Best Spin Win

    comments, called-out
    + Comment now
    + Comment now

    The 57th quadrennial presidential election which takes place on Tuesday November 6th should be riveting theater, as many national election projections (e.g. CNN, NBC/Wall Street Journal) are predicting an extremely close race in both the electoral college and popular vote.

    Most of us already know who we are going to vote for, but that doesn’t stop politicians from bombarding us with commercial after commercial…most of which spend more time attacking the opponent than highlighting the merits of the candidate.

    This is because politics itself is sport.  Its rivals trying to win your vote, and well-funded candidates often have the edge because their campaign can afford greater marketing and advertising resources to spread their message.

    It is my perception that the degree of voter disenchantment with mudslinging campaign tactics seems to reach higher and higher levels each election year, as often times the salacious advertisements do more to confuse the issues and misrepresent facts than bring key matters to light.

    And the side that produces the best spin is the side that usually wins.  Especially when that spin revolves around the public’s perceived health of the overall economy.

    During a recent class on labor economics in which we discussed the historical relationship between the health of the economy and election trends, the presence of spin doctoring is on full display when we look at how both the Obama administration and Governor Romney explain their perspective on what has happened to the economy during President Obama’s first term.

    As background to this debate, let’s focus on the national unemployment rate which is considered a lagging indicator of GDP.  Over the last 10 years, we see 3 distinct time periods:

    (1) Unemployment fell from a local high of 6.3% in June 2003 to a local min of 4.4% as recently as May 2007;

    (2) Then unemployment rose from 4.4% in May 2007 to a local high of 10.0% by October 2009;

    (3) Unemployment has fallen since October 2009, reaching a local min of 7.8% in September 2012.

    Meanwhile, recall that (a) George W. Bush was elected President in November 2000, (b) was re-elected in November 2004, and (c) President Obama was elected in November 2008.

    Armed with this information, let’s see how the spin doctoring takes place.

    If you are Barack Obama’s political strategist…

    You argue that:

    - The increases in unemployment during the first 10 months of your tenure as commander-in-chief could not have been avoided, and were the product of negative lags in the economy which emanated from the end of President Bush’s 2nd term in office.

    - When fixing the economy with either fiscal or monetary policy, time lags between problem identification, solution implementation, and policy impact are known to range in time between 6 months to 3 years…with longer lags associated with fiscal policy, which is the type of policy that the President has greater influence over.  And the data reflects that the unemployment rate started reversing itself within 10 months of the beginning of the administration…and has steadily improved ever since.
