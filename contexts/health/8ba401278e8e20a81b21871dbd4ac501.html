CIPD 2012: Staff engagement reveals ‘health of your business’

United Utilities benefits from increased engagement
Staff engagement is important to get right because it is “an assessment of the health of your business from the bottom up”, a senior HR professional told delegates at the CIPD’s annual conference in Manchester.

Louise Beardmore, head of organisational development and HR operations at United Utilities, explained how her company had become the “most improved water company” in just two years by changing its approach to staff engagement.

The firm, which supplies water to the North West of England, had experienced low engagement scores of 38 per cent. “We had huge amounts of disengagement,” she said, “but now 83 per cent of the workforce feel engaged. However, there is no silver bullet to improving engagement. It is about keeping it simple.”

Beardmore explained that she started to turn the scores around by listening to staff through the twice-yearly staff survey. Previously this had yielded low response rates because it was sent out by email. “We have a geographically dispersed workforce and most of our employees spend their time in sewers or vans, so they weren’t going to fill in an email survey. So we put 85 per cent of the survey back on paper and asked people to fill them in during their team meetings and send them back anonymously. This put the response rates at above 90 per cent.”

In addition to this, Beardmore said the firm went on the offensive to get all employees and managers to take part in improving engagement. One HR initiative that supported this drive was ‘team workout’, which gave local teams the autonomy to “solve the problems they had highlighted in the survey themselves", she said.

“We gave them 90 days of empowerment and support to do what they thought would fix the issue they’d told us about,” she explained. She said that rises in customer satisfaction correlated with rises in engagement, which had contributed to the firm’s “most improved water company” accolade.

But she warned delegates to ask for staff feedback only if they were prepared to act on it, as inaction can drive disillusionment and disengagement. Beardmore also warned HR not to get “analysis paralysis” when scores are over-analysed.

She explained that many of her line managers come from an engineering background and wanted her to prove the value of engagement. “To avoid ‘analysis paralysis’, I asked them to think about which members of their team were most engaged in their role and then think about how much time they had to spend with each person. This makes the value of engagement clearer because it’s clear that work contributions from engaged people are much higher.”

However, even the best people strategy would not work if the line manager “selling it” to their teams was not engaged with it, she told the conference. Beardmore said line managers were encouraged to take ownership of engagement by reporting to HR what action they had taken as a result of feedback from staff. “Asking for this feedback helped to drive change in behaviour and is the most important target, not the scorecard,” Beardmore said.
