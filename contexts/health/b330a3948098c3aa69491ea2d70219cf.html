Path to wellness: Sprinkle Avenue community improves health during study

Print Email
2012-11-06T04:00:00Z Path to wellness: Sprinkle Avenue community improves health during studyBy DIONNE GLEATON, T&D Staff Writer The Times and Democrat
8 hours ago  •  By DIONNE GLEATON, T&D Staff Writer
(0) Comments

Jessica Hook has been living with HIV for two decades. SheÕs also battling gout, diabetes, osteoporosis and asthma, but said a University of South Carolina study has helped keep her on a path to healthier living.

ÒSome people donÕt get any attention until something like that comes along, so that gives them a great piece of hope and encouragement,Ó Hook said of the study.

Developing coalitions to promote health education and connect residents to free or low-cost health care to manage chronic disease was the goal of the two-year trial study, called Positive Action for TodayÕs Health, in OrangeburgÕs Sprinkle Avenue community. Orangeburg was one of three communities included in the health psychology study, which USC also conducted in Florence and Sumter.

Findings from the study, funded by the National Institutes of Health, appear in the latest issue of the American Journal of Public Health.

Dawn Wilson, a psychology professor at USC, conducted the study, which showed that a communityÕs grassroots social marketing campaigns were effective in implementing successful walking programs in impoverished communities. While Sumter and Florence had police-patrolled walking programs, with Sumter also adding a social marketing component to their design, Orangeburg was charged with developing a comprehensive health promotion program that ran from January 2009 to January 2011.

ÒThey came up with the idea of building a coalition that included community, church, health care organizations and administrative leaders throughout Orangeburg who were interested in preventing chronic disease and helping to manage chronic disease issues in the Sprinkle Avenue area,Ó Wilson said.

Community liaison Horace Britton, project coordinator Barry Fogle and the Rev. Samuel Hughes, pastor of the Ministry of Reconciliation in Orangeburg, were among community members lending support. A steering committee was comprised of local residents, as well as representatives from the Regional Medical Center, the Orangeburg-Calhoun Free Medical Clinic, the Orangeburg County Health Department, Victory Tabernacle Church and the Minority AIDS Council.

ÒThe communityÕs biggest concern was that folks were coming out of the hospital after heart surgery or a stroke and not having follow up ... to help them through chronic disease events,Ó Wilson said. ÒThe vision was to develop a station where individuals could come and talk to somebody about their health issues, get resources and information, and then have somebody help them get back into follow-up care ... and make decisions on how to manage their health after chronic disease.Ó

Health screenings for diabetes and hypertension, and health seminars on topics such as nutrition, cancer prevention and HIV/AIDS were held in the community.

ÒIt was good to get that kind of information because at the time, I was not a diabetic patient, but now I am,Ó said Hook, who was given a pedometer to monitor her walking activity. ÒAll that knowledge I got there comes in good now that IÕve been living with diabetes for the past seven months.Ó

Wilson said the study followed a group of nearly 100 Orangeburg residents who had their blood pressure checked every six months and gave updates on their overall health.

ÒThat data is what weÕre continuing to write reports on,Ó she said. ÒWeÕll be able to tell more about whether weÕll be able to reduce rates of hypertension and diabetes. Seventy-five percent of participants that were recruited for us to follow their health status stayed with us the entire two years.

ÒIt was hard to have the program end, but at the same time, all the communities were willing to keep going toward their own vision.Ó

Fogle called the program Òa great success.Ó

ÒIt helped the community members with their blood pressure and helped them to also eat right,Ó he said. ÒWe also had a dentist to come out. It made a difference. People are watching and checking blood pressure. I think it has had a very positive impact on the community.Ó

Britton said the promotion of preventive care was key in helping to keep people from becoming ill in the first place.

ÒWe also had speakers to come in to talk about heart disease and stroke. We had someone to come in to also talk about community safety and HIV/AIDS,Ó Britton said. ÒI know that the project served to teach people how to be healthy and the importance of taking control of their own health. I know some people are doing stuff on their own, and thatÕs what weÕve encourage people to do as they move toward healthy living, in terms of eating and exercise.Ó

What is on the horizon for the community?

ÒDoing more education seminars and possibly health screenings,Ó Britton said. ÒWe may have something planned for the Orangeburg Area Development Center in that area at a future date.Ó

Minority AIDS Council president Shirley James said she Òprovided emphasis on the existing health care services in the Orangeburg areas as a way to help sustain the impact that the project would have on health care.Ó

ÒI encouraged health care/health education organizations and partners to join the community efforts by providing resources, support and health screenings during planned health events and wellness fairs,Ó she said. ÒI want to continue to assist the Sprinkle Avenue communityÕs efforts.Ó
