Flu Season Health Precautions

By TEDDY WAYNE

Anxiety: We worry. A gallery of contributors count the ways.

Tags:

Anxiety, health, humor, influenza

It’s flu season, and an ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure. But don’t worry — just follow these preventative measures to ensure a healthy winter!

Cover your mouth when you cough. Sneeze into your shoulder. If you sneeze again, sneeze into your opposite shoulder. If you’re one of those people who always sneezes three times in a row, you’re really annoying.

If someone you work with exhibits flu-like symptoms, refrain from shaking his hand, avoid his cubicle in human resources, and spread rumors about him and the redhead from accounts receivable at the September company retreat.

Wash your hands immediately when you enter the house. Use toilet paper to turn on the faucets, even in your own bathroom. Use another piece of toilet paper to extract the faucet-turning toilet paper. Whatever you do, never touch the toilet-paper spindle. After two such uses, build a new bathroom.

Inspect the heads of your school-age children for lice hourly. When they complain you’re checking them too often, respond, “Hey, maybe I have lice,” and give them a comforting parental wink before tucking them into bed. In the middle of the night, give them lice. To prove your point further the next day, hide the lice shampoo.

Your computer keyboard is one of the dirtiest areas in the house. Go back to your old typewriter. Once that becomes filthy, haul out your old printing press. After that, get your obsolete first-generation Kindle.

Kristian Hammerstad

When hiking in forested areas, wear loose, light clothing that covers your entire body, unlike the tight, revealing, all-black outfits worn by whatshername from accounts receivable.

Studies show that overuse of antibacterial soap may, ironically, render you more susceptible to bacteria. Therefore, wash your hands in bacteria to render yourself invulnerable to antibacterial soap. I’m blowing your mind, aren’t I?

If you own a pet, bathe it regularly, speak to it in a baby voice, and ponder what voids a nonverbal, completely dependent animal is filling in your life.

Triple-wash spinach, even if you’re not eating any and are just spotting some wild spinach on a forested-area hike during a company retreat that’s been completely ruined by two coworkers’ inappropriate flirtations. Destroy your complete widescreen DVD collection of “Popeye” cartoons. Start a blog ranting against the potent spinach lobby. Become iron-deficiency anemic.

E-mail every health article you read to your entire contact list with the subject line “Thought this might interest you! ” Include links to YouTube videos of cats engaging in un-catlike behavior (playing the piano, snuggling with dogs, filing quarterly self-employment taxes, etc.).

Keep a bottle of hand sanitizer on you at all times. But, wait, it’s the same problem as the toilet paper — the sanitizer bottle itself is filthy, because you’ve been handling it with your dirty fingers. So get a second sanitizer bottle to clean the first one off. Then a third bottle to clean the second, and on and on, until you are sued for plagiarism of “The Cat in the Hat Comes Back” by the estate of Dr. Seuss.

Cook meat well. Serve it on a fluffy bed of couscous with basil for garnish, accompanied by a robust French red from any odd year. Have strained conversation about work and your kids with your ostensible good friends with whom you no longer have much in common and your passionless middle-manager husband. At a lull, smile nervously and say, “Isn’t this well-cooked and therefore hygienic-to-consume meat?” At the end of the night, air-kiss your departing guests from behind the safety of the shower curtain as you grout new tiles.

Don’t refill plastic bottles. Instead, fill them with pennies. When a bottle becomes full, empty it, then refill it with tap water, which will now have a zesty coppery taste and reduce your various mineral deficiencies.

Wear gloves when riding public transportation. Also wear eye goggles, a HazMat suit, and, because you never know if a certain H.R. rep will be on board, a pair of really cute strappy sandals.

Do not socialize with ailing members of the Greatest Generation, the financially anxious Baby Boomers, the Gen X slackers, the Gen Y social-networking addicts, the even lazier Gen Z children of the Gen X slackers, the future Gen A iCyborg progeny of Gen Y, anyone who has played in the last three years for the New York Mets, and people who nod and respond to everything with “Totally!”

It is well known that reading about sickness, especially on the Internet, can lead to hypochondria and, in fact, weaken the immune system from the stress of imagining the symptoms that might befall you. Therefore, never read about sickness on the Internet, particularly if you are already an anxious person. To repeat: reading about sickness on the Internet will only compound your anxiety.

Concoct a scheme to give the girl in accounts receivable lice.

Teddy Wayne is the author of the novels “Kapitoil” and the forthcoming “The Love Song of Jonny Valentine.” He teaches fiction writing at Marymount Manhattan College.
