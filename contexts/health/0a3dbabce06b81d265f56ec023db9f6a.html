Health Wonk Review: Wearing the Green for the St. Patrick’s Day Edition

March 15, 2012 — by Tinker Ready

Here in Boston,Êresearchers have looked into that most pressing of St. PatrickÕs Day health questions: Is Guinness really good for you? Red wine gets all the press, but Tufts researchers found a positive association between beer and bone density.

For this edition of the Health Wonk Review, we take aÊa look atÊthe Irish Times and find thatÊeven with a national health plan, Ireland has to deal with barriers to care.ÊOne story detailsÊslow progress in the establishment of promised primary care centers, and another story, asks ÒIs the EU good for your health?Ó

So,Êwhile we refight the battle over birth control, the Irish health ministryÊ– of the largely Catholic country — is releasing a Òsexual healthÓ app.

Back here in the new world,Êthe great grandchildrenÊofÊIrish immigrants celebrate theirÊheritage asÊLinda Leu at the Health Access Blog writes about aÊreport that “highlights the need for cultural competency and language access, to welcome all ethnicities from California (and America’s) diverse communtiiesÉ.The Importance of Equity in the Bridge to Reform: As St. Patrick’s Day celebrates Irish ethnic pride, we need to take note of the full diversity of our states and nation. As we get ready for 2014, remember the communities that will be newly served may not look (or speak) the same as those that the current system serves.”Ê

For theÊonce-a-yearÊstep dancers,Gary Schwitzer at Health News Review answers questions about outcomes data on knee replacement surgery.ÊAlso see posts on the JGIM paper onÊÊdata onÊ shared decision-making in prostate cancer surgery & coronary stents decisionsÊ Êand an online ÒDaily DealÓ coupon for preventive MRI scan Ð disease-mongering du jour

BostonÕs own David Williams notes: ÒProfits are up at Massachusetts health plans –should you be upset? On his Health Business Blog, he writes: The Globe reports higher profits and CEO compensation at Massachusetts health plans. But profit margins are low and if anything the CEOs are underpaid.”

More of the best of recent health policy posts

At The Hospitalist Leader, BradÊFlansbaum examines physician pay,Òfairness, and how it relates to the reinvigoration of primary care.ÊSpecialty physicians take note.Ó

Another post on health careÊproviders — and theirÊsupport teamsÊ– notes: ÒThere’s been much discussion of the potential impact of health reform, aka Obamacare, on employmentÓÊ Joseph Paduda at ManagedCareMatters.comÊwrites: ÒMost has referenced employers cutting jobs to avoid the mandate or save dollars for premiums. ÊWhat hasn’t received much attention are the new jobs – mostly high-paying ones- that will be created as more Americans are insured and seek coverage and care.Ó

Julie Ferguson of WorkersÕ Comp Insider asks ÒIf you had to guess what workplace experienced most assaults by customers/patronsÊwhat would you guess? If you guessed healthcare, you would be right.” She informs us that 61% of all workplace assaults are committed by healthcare patients, according to a recent report issued by NCCI.

Two posts came in on shady practices.

Colorado Health Insurance Insider offers: Colorado AG Files Lawsuit Against Discount and Mini-Med Health Plan: Ê”ItÕs a pretty typical website for that sort of product, with lots of great-sounding claims and sample cases where members have supposedly saved thousands of dollars. ÊBut they also have a link for people who want to Òbecome a resellerÓ. ÊAnd their process of getting recruits enrolled to sell the product is what has come under the watchful eye of the Colorado AG.”

ÊCalling it ÒThe latest example of misbehavior by a large health care corporation,Ó ÊRoy M. Poses at Health Care Renewal writes : Gentiva’s Odyssey Healthcare Settles Again, Signs Yet Another Corporate Integrity AgreementÊand gets “little more than a financial wrist slap.Ê The case was about allegations that a for-profit hospice enrolled patients who did not meet the regulations for federal reimbursement for hospice care.Ê In particular, they were alleged to be patients who really did not seem to have extremely limited life expectancies.Ê It is true that enrolling such patients lead the government to pay more for their care than might otherwise be the case.Ê But the real problem is that patients may have been denied treatments that could have improved, or even lengthened their lives.Ê

Two on workplace wellness:

ÒWellness Program Implementation at WCS Looks a Lot Like Dating” says Kat Haselkorn Corporate Wellness Insights. This post details the similarities betweenÊcustomer satisfactionÊand romantic relationships. Although wellness program implementation and dating do not seem to have much in common, we have found that the process of making a client happy mirrors the act of keeping a romantic prospect satisfied. When it comes to setting up a wellness program, we do whatever it takes!

Henry Stern, LUTCF,Ê at the CBC InsureBlogÊwrites about ÒHealth vs Common $ense, challenging Òthe conventional wisdom that workplace health promotion programs work.Ó

And two on HIT:

ÒCompetition today in healthcare encourages care providers to hoardÊpatient data.Ó ÊsaysÊVince Kuraitis of the Êe-CareManagement blog in a post called ÒStage 2 MU Rules : The proposed Stage 2 Meaningful Use rulesÊsupportÊmoving competitionÊin healthcare to the rightÊbases Ñ sharing and adding value to patient health record data.

Health Affairs offers a post byÊDanny McCormick, of Harvard Medical School and the Cambridge Heath Alliance, and coauthors David Bor, Stephanie Woolhandler, and David Himmelstein. The title of the post is Ê“The Effect Of PhysiciansÕ Electronic Access To Tests: A Response To FarzadÊMostashariÓ

The four authors of the post are also the authors of an article in the March issueÊof Health Affairs. The article reported that electronic access to computerized imaging results (either the report or the actual image) by physicians was associated with a 40% -70% increase in imaging tests, including sharp increases in expensive tests like MRIsÊand CT scans; the findings for blood tests were similar. The article prompted a critical blog post by national health IT coordinator FarzadÊMostashari.

Jason Shafrin writes: ÒAmericans are aÊlitigiousÊculture.Ê The malpractice claims that make it to court, however, are not many as you may think.The Healthcare Economist explains why.

Finally, how does health care In the U.S. compare to other countries? ÊOnÊthe Disease Management Care BlogÊ Dr. Jaan Sidorov tests our knowledge of and finds: We’re not so bad after all!

So, our days of drinking black — or greenÊ — beer are over. But, on Saturday I’ll boil up some cabbage and — sorry Walter Willett –Êand a hunk of Êcorned beef in honor of my Irish nanny and theÊReadysÊand GradysÊ and MurphysÊwho left theirÊgreen shores andÊmade theirÊwayÊacross the sea to settle in BostonÊand New York.Ê Happy St. Patrick’s Day from BHN.
