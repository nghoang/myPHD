Health Alerts: Workplace ban lowers heart attack rate, study shows

WOUNDS

Triple-layer, tubular bandage works better on chronic wounds

New research could help the roughly 6.5 million people in the United States who suffer from chronic wounds.

Chronic wounds won’t heal, or heal slowly, cause significant pain and often return. Scientists have found that tubular compression bandaging is much more effective in healing such wounds and cost about half of standard compression bandages.

Researchers tested the use of three-layer, compression bandages — similar to socks — on hospital clinic patients with venous leg ulcers, one of the most common and painful types of chronic wounds, which occur when leg veins fail to pump enough oxygenated blood.

The tubular compression bandages helped wounds heal better, were still comfortable and cut the risk of recurrence compared with standard, one-layer compression bandages.

Results appear in Wound Repair and Regeneration.

LEARN MORE: www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed, search for “tubular compression”

SOURCES: Monash University in Australia; U.S. National Library of Medicine

SMOKING

Workplace ban lowers heart attack rate, study shows

A new study has found the strongest evidence yet that smoke-free workplace laws that reduce secondhand smoke inhalation can lead to reductions in heart attacks.

Research by scientists at the Mayo Clinic in Rochester, Minn., found a 33 percent drop in heart attack rates in a Minnesota county after enactment of public smoking bans.

“This should turn the page on the chapter discussing whether or not secondhand smoke is a risk factor for heart attacks,” said Dr. Richard D. Hurt, an author of the study and a professor of medicine at Mayo.

Published in Archives of Internal Medicine, the study examined medical data in Olmsted County, which has a population of about 144,000, over two periods: the 18 months before the county banned smoking in restaurants in 2002 and the 18 months after it extended the ban to bars and all workplaces in 2007. Hurt and his colleagues found that while rates of hypertension, diabetes, high cholesterol and obesity remained constant or increased after the bans, the incidence of heart attacks dropped sharply.

LEARN MORE: smokefree.gov, lung.org/stop-smoking

The New York Times

HEART HEALTH

Watermelon juice: It was great for mice

Eating watermelon might help people trying to lose weight and improve heart health.

A new study discovered that mice on high-fat, high-cholesterol diets that were given watermelon juice gained less weight than a control group, cut their bad cholesterol level in half and decreased arterial plaque by about the same amount.

Purdue University and University of Kentucky researchers were trying to determine whether citrulline, an amino acid found in watermelon, lowered blood pressure. They did not find that, but mice given water containing 2 percent watermelon juice gained 30 percent less weight than mice that received the same amount of water plus a solution equal to the carbohydrates in the watermelon juice.

Researchers for the study, which appears in the Journal of Nutritional Biochemistry, plan more research on citrulline’s impact on health.

LEARN MORE: heart.org, search “healthy eating;” ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed, search “citrulline watermelon”

SOURCES: Purdue University, Journal of Nutritional Biochemistry
