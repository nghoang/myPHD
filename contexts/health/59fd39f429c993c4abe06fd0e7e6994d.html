Expert Analysis

  • Crisis Management From The Board's Perspective

    Companies should be prepared to develop an accurate, consistent and thoughtful message in a timely fashion during a crisis. Communications and legal experts should be consulted as messages that make sense from a public relations may be disastrous from a legal liability perspective, say Keir Gumbs and Xiaowen Qian of Covington & Burling LLP.

  • Another Blow To OFCCP's Grab For Health Care Providers

    The nearly four-year effort by the Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs to expand its jurisdiction over health care providers based on their participation in the Department of Defense TRICARE program has suffered another significant blow — OFCCP v. Florida Hospital of Orlando. Unfortunately, the decision leaves open the possibility that some arrangements under TRICARE could support OFCCP jurisdiction, says David Goldstein of Littler Mendelson PC.

  • How The Presidential Election Could Curtail ADA Regs

    The outcome of this presidential election could have a significant impact on the development of two very important Americans with Disabilities Act regulations under development by the U.S. Department of Justice that would apply, essentially, to businesses and nonprofit organizations that provide goods and services to the public. If Gov. Romney wins, it will be interesting to see how his pro-business approach manifests in these pending regulations, says Minh Vu of Seyfarth Shaw LLP.

  • Mapping The Pre-Election Landscape

    Both parties are giving it everything they've got as we enter the final stages of the 2012 presidential campaign. The conventional wisdom among electoral vote counters is that it could be difficult for Governor Romney to find his way to 270 electoral votes without Ohio. It could be difficult for President Obama as well, but he has more scenarios under which he could win the election without Ohio's support, say attorneys with SNR Denton.

  • Missing Marchers In The HHS Breach Parade

    Much has been written about the agreement of Massachusetts Eye and Ear Infirmary to pay the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services $1.5 million to settle potential violations involving an alleged security breach in 2010 of protected health information. But this is only the second of what may be three significant breaches experienced by MEEI in the last three years. It is perplexing that nothing about the organization's 2012 breach has been posted on the HHS list of breaches to this point, says Michael Kline of Fox Rothschild LLP.

  • Look Before You Leap Into A Securities Litigation Trap

    The possibility of expiring tax cuts combined with mandatory reductions in federal spending at year end have prompted observers to warn that the U.S. faces a “fiscal cliff” over which the economy might plunge into another recession. In this climate, public companies can be sure that plaintiffs’ lawyers will be on the lookout for targets of “stock drop” securities fraud class action litigation. But the securities laws are not without protection for companies that assess fiscal cliff risks and disclose them if they are material, say attorneys with Choate Hall & Stewart LLP.

  • Opportunities And Challenges Of Telemedicine

    The use of technology and telecommunications for the delivery of health care services when the health care practitioner and patient are not in the same physical location is growing in popularity across the nation. Although telemedicine can offer significant benefits to local providers and their patients, all parties must be aware of the associated challenges and take steps to address the regulatory and common law issues that may impact such services, says Vanessa Reynolds of Broad & Cassel.

  • Corporate Profits Don’t Amount To DOJ’s Gain Under FCA

    In this era of widespread government success in the False Claims Act arena, the Sixth Circuit decision in Williams v. Renal Care Group Inc. provides some comfort to FCA defendants that have begun to feel they have little recourse as the U.S. Department of Justice seeks to employ overly aggressive or unfounded theories of liability. The message could not have been clearer — legitimate attempts to maximize profits based on a reasonable interpretation of government regulations does not give rise to FCA liability, say attorneys with Vinson & Elkins LLP.

  • Nonprofit Health Systems Ride The Merger Wave

    The dramatic increase in merger activity among nonprofit hospitals and health systems is prompting a new set of governance and transaction issues to be addressed by corporate counsel and other advisers. A unique feature of this current wave of transaction activity is the renewed popularity of the nonprofit collaboration option, say Michael Peregrine of McDermott Will & Emery LLP and Dr. David Nygren of Nygren Consulting LLC.

  • In Support Of Learned Intermediary Doctrine

    The New Jersey Appellate Division’s decision in three Hoffmann-La Roche Inc. cases provides important support for pharmaceutical manufacturers defending failure-to-warn claims under Florida law in New Jersey and elsewhere. Manufacturers facing failure-to-warn product litigation should evaluate whether the learned intermediary doctrine is a viable defense to causation and the necessary requirements to assert such a defense, says Mimi Dennis of Sedgwick LLP.
