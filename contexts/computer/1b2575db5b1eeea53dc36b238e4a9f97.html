Which ‘HT’ to Treat: Hypertension or Hammertoe?

By ABIGAIL ZUGER, M.D.
Published: October 8, 2012

You can jump into a swimming pool, you can dive, or you can inch across the shallow end, making faces and shrieking. But no matter how you immerse yourself, you’ve still got to swim once you’re in.

The Digital Doctor

In this special issue of Science Times, we look at some of the many ways that technology is changing the world of medicine.

And as with cold blue water, so with the electronic medical record. The faster the plunge, the briefer the initial pain, but once that first shock is over you still need to move and breathe in a whole new way.

An electronic medical record is not just a neatened-up version of a paper chart. Instead, it recreates the entire health care universe, integrating all of it, from woes to bills, into one glorious paper-free whole.

Not surprisingly, it changes every part of the medical workday, from where you sit and what you do to which glasses you wear to work. From Day 1 you can feel the thing nuzzling at your brain — its whole purpose, of course, is to change the way you think, forcing you willy-nilly down predetermined paths, whether they are actually the right ones or not.

In its fully realized form (one in which it stops crashing, learns some basic medical logic, gets its default drug doses right and successfully compiles all relevant clinical information into a single searchable database) the electronic record could probably be a wonderful thing.

But that incarnation is still largely mythical. Instead, most clinicians are dealing with lesser versions, an experience not unlike slowly traversing the shallow end of a very large pool: Half in and half out, you can neither walk nor swim but just stumble along in chilly misery.

Dr. Tracy Voss, an internist at the University of South Carolina in Columbia and a colleague of a friend of mine, had a fairly typical initial plunge. A month after electronic records went live at her practice, her posture one morning said it all: In complete violation of both ergonomic and clinical principles, she faced neither computer nor patient but swiveled rapidly between the two while flipping through a thick paper chart held open on a flexed knee.

The great digital transition day had been preceded by weeks of computer training and celebrated with a tray of breakfast pastries provided by very cheerful representatives of the software company. Now Dr. Voss and her colleagues were on their own, wading past all the usual emotional landmarks.

There was tear-stained panic: That was on the first day, when the new laptops refused to recognize passwords. A flick of their wireless switches fixed that.

There was denial: All phone messages were now supposed to be typed into the computer, but the really urgent ones were still scribbled on a paper towel and slid under the recipient’s nose.

There was nostalgia: Dr. Voss especially grieved for the little pictures she used to draw in her charts to explain blood pressure readings. “Everything on the screen looks the same,” she said.

There was low comedy: A doctor used “HT” to mean “hypertension,” but the computer understood “hammertoe.” Confusion ensued.

And finally, there was the miserable marsh of digital mismatch. Dr. Voss’s new computer could not retrieve the report she needed from another computer 100 yards away in the hospital next door. She spent 20 minutes tinkering to no avail; finally a nurse called for the result, noted it on a paper towel, put in under Dr. Voss’s nose.

It is possible Dr. Voss and her colleagues will eventually experience some version of smooth sailing with their electronic records. It is also possible they will stay in transition for far longer than they would like, as many of us have.

At our hospital, for reasons none of us know but duly assume must be good, we have been stuck at the shallow end of the digital revolution for many years. It is not a pleasant place to be, although a prime vantage point for assessing the terrain.

Doctors still write in paper charts, which means you spend forever on the wards trying to find the charts you need, and then have problems reading everyone’s handwriting, including your own. Still, you can write what you think, a privilege widely underestimated until it is revoked. On the horizon are systems that will urge us to click our way down a giant menu of stock phrases to create digital notes, which will be fabulously legible and give readers exactly no idea of who patients are and how they are doing.

Multiple computer systems are still running simultaneously. Some always talk to one another, some never do, and some have periodic, inexplicable fallings-out, during which valuable information becomes unavailable save by the old standbys: Call the lab, or head down there on foot.

Even in its most primitive form, the strengths of the electronic record are clear. Not for nothing has it been dubbed by some cynics the “electronic cash register”: Aggressive billing to wring all possible cash from each clinical encounter becomes breathtakingly easy.

The same holds for prescriptions: Just a few clicks and you have written a dozen effortlessly. Click again six months later and you have refilled them all. You never even have to think about the fact that very few people should be taking a dozen drugs over the long haul.

Electronic records clearly let one practice a certain form of medicine very well. One might argue that it is the lowest possible form of our poor battered profession. Still, there is no turning back, and so we wade ahead, ever hopeful, toward the distant shore.

Dr. Abigail Zuger, an infectious-disease physician in Manhattan, is the health book reviewer for Science Times.
