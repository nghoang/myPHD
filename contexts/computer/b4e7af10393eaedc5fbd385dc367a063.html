Sock Puppets Rule the World

Friday, April 2, 2010 at 9:25AM

Kerri Leonard, Christopher Greenslate and a whole host of wonderfully well-employed cheerleaders for healthy eating, continue to amaze me. "Sugar and processed foods are bad!" they chant relentlessly, "growing your own vegetables is good!"

The problem is, very few of the vegan/organic/natural foods/third-world poverty mavens ever give up their cars, commutes, teaching positions (love those 2-3 months off every summer to cultivate the garden without the interruption of any pesky job), big mortgages, and generally inefficient lifestyles. Oh they're all trying mightily no doubt about it but failing miserably to achieve change. Third-world poverty? Those bad, bad "managers" of those bad, bad countries just aren't arranging to feed the nice, starving citizens. Wow let's send 'em another 50 boatloads of food and cash (simultaneously fattening the wallets of the corrupt charlatans at the U.N. and the black marketeers). Economic recession in the U.S.? Bah no worries eat on a dollar-a-day! Go on you can do it! Give up steak and eggs before you give up your car or sell the house and buy something smaller and more affordable? Damn right! You consume too much (food that is), so the solution is to eat bird seed, keep the fuel-guzzling car, keep the mortgage and rally at every conceivable opportunity against the crepuscular, infected and rapacious agri-business industrial machine? Okay. Sure. It just hasn't done much good so far.

But for the fact that 99% of the people on the planet have neither the time nor the arable land to grow so much as a single zucchini, it would all make sense. But for the fact that contemporary governments have colluded with global business to burden the citizens of almost every country on the planet with bad decisions, cross-purposed planning, and power driven not by merit but by stock prices, life might actually be bearable and livable without periodic rages expressed at the maundering satraps we continually elect to run the whole show.

So I've concluded that Sock Puppets rule the world.

Leonard and Greenslate may be commended for their good intentions, for the funds they periodically raise to help feed impoverished families, and for the book they've written which might even help a few families learn to eat affordably (if they can find the fifteen bucks plus shipping to order the book from Amazon or Barnes & Noble in the first place). Problem is, these sock puppets don't directly address the fundamental problem of living beyond our means.

So how is your family budget? How is your business budget? Are you making the same mistake as Leonard and Greenslate sacrificing nutrition (in other words, the thing which is fundamental to a successfully thriving existence) in order to save money? In business, we have to consider every purchase, upgrade, new process and new employee very carefully indeed. So what sort of car are you leasing? Are you paying $1,000 a month for luxury when you could be paying $350 for just really nice? Is the computer sitting on your desk more powerful than the one on your Admin's desk (you know, the Admin who actually does need the fastest and most reliable computer in the whole business)?

Don't be a sock puppet. Get your priorities straight.

Sony Vaio All-in-Ones

Thursday, September 4, 2008 at 6:12PM

Sony has introduced a trio of all-in-one Vaios - the JS, LV and RT. These babies not only handle word processing, design, web browsing and other computing tasks, but they also work great as small, if expensive, HDTVs. All units have support for Blu-ray Discs either optionally or as standard in the top line models.

The VAIO JS All-in-One desktop chassis features a 20.1? LCD display with Sonys XBRITE-ECO LCD technology. It will be available in black, silver and pink colors and feature an integrated microphone and web cam with face-tracking technology. It will have an SRP starting at around $1,000.

The Vaio LV, features floating mirror design and has a 24? WUXGA (1,920 x 1,200 resolution) widescreen high definition display using Sonys own XBRITE-FullHD LCD technology. It also comes standard with a slot-in rewritable Blu-ray Disc optical drive, and has integrated DVR capabilities, up to 1TB of storage space, an HDMI input, Wi-Fi connectivity and an integrated power supply, allowing for an uncluttered wallmount. The VAIO LV have an SRP starting at around $1,600.

The Vaio RT High Definition Studio features a 25.5? LCD XBRITE-FullHD LCD technology Display, a Blu-ray optical drive, integrated digital TV tuners, and an HDMI port. TheVAIO RT High-Definition Studio PC will start at about $3,300.
