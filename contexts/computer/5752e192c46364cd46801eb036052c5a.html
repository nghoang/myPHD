Do Student Computer Deals Make the Grade?

Deal of the Day: Early electronics sales for students may prove an expensive lesson.

By KELLI B. GRANT

Today marks the first official day of summer, and electronics retailers already have their eye on back-to-school shoppers. But early shoppers this year could end up earning an "F" in bargain hunting.

Apple, Best Buy and Dell are among the companies that already announced their lineup of computer deals for college-bound students. Apple, for example, offers Mac buyers a $100 gift card usable toward book, app, music and other media purchases, while Dell bundles PCs with the buyer's choice of a free 4GB Xbox 360 (worth $200) or a $200 eGift card for Dell.com. Best Buy also offers a free Xbox for students who spend at least $699 on a Windows laptop, among other deals.

Tempting though the deals may appear, experts say most students should hold off. Parents and students, they point out, can wait until at least late July and still grab those deals; Apple's deal, for example, lasts until Sept. 21. Plus, consumers may see prices drop even further. Last year, laptop prices in August were 13% cheaper than in June, factoring in all available sales and deals, according to DealNews.com. "These companies are fishing for suckers with back-to-school sales in June," says Dan de Grandpre, chief executive of sale-tracking site DealNews.com.

Experts predict more laptops are expected to be priced below the $300 mark later this summer than now, and $600 to $700 ultrabooks could drop to $500. Even the new MacBook Air is likely to get cheaper through retailer offers. By the time back-to-school is at its peak in August, consumers will see the best deals, says de Grandpre.

Another layer of opportunity: a handful of states offer summer sales-tax holidays in late July and early August. (So far, states including Virginia, Texas, Maryland and Iowa plan to offer them.) Eligible items often include computers as well as extras like printers and software, says Carol Kokinis-Graves, a senior analyst with tax advisory firm CCH. States typically consider an item eligible even if it's on sale, or purchased online. So a shopper buying a new laptop could take advantage of the ongoing Dell, Best Buy or Apple deals and avoid paying tax to boot. The average tax savings is a about 4%, says Kokinis-Graves.

Some manufacturers -- including Apple, Dell, HP and Sony -- also offer a student discount year round to shoppers who buy directly from them. Shoppers should factor in that education pricing when comparing available deals, says technology consultant Alex Goldfayn, the author of "Evangelist Marketing." Manufacturers typically offer current students and their parents a lower price of laptops, software and other items when they buy direct. At Apple, for example, students can snag a new MacBook Air for as little as $949, $50 less than what other shoppers see. That makes Apple's offer of $100 toward apps, books, etc., more valuable than Best Buy's $100 discount on select Macs. Dell sells students the Inspiron 17R laptop for $750 instead of $850.

One exception to the waiting game, experts say: recent graduates. Offers usually require proof of student status, including a valid student ID and a .edu email address. New grads may have both, but not for much longer: most colleges transfer graduates over to an alumni email address shortly after commencement. "Educational pricing is one of the biggest discounts," he says, and so those who will soon be considered non-students are better off grabbing it while they can instead of gambling on later sales.
