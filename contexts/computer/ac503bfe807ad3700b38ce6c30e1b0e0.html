Broken computer stalls voting at Ann Arbor Community Center

BY ADAM RUBENFIRE AND JOSEPH LICHTERMAN

A malfunctioning electronic device brought voting at the Ann Arbor Community Center to a standstill Tuesday morning.

Some voters at the Community Center said they waited in line for nearly two hours in order to vote. Lines at the center started in the parking lot, reaching the sidewalk on North Main Street, and snaked through the building. Election officials moved more people waiting to vote inside the building because of the chilly temperatures.

Ed Golembiewski, director of elections for Washtenaw county, said he spoke with Ann Arbor City Clerk Jacqueline Beaudry Tuesday morning, and was advised that an electronic pollbook Ñ a device that allows poll workers to process voter information Ñ at the community center was malfunctioning. In response, the precinctÕs chairperson decided to stop processing voters.

Golembiewski pointed out that there are backup procedures when electronic voting devices malfunction. He said poll workers can use a paper pollbook in lieu of the computer.

ÒThere are ways to continue the voting process without an electronic pollbook,Ó Golembiewski said. ÒThatÕs what the city clerkÕs office is implementing.Ó

Golembiewski said the city clerkÕs office sent two staff members and an IT expert to remedy the problem, and voter processing has resumed. He added that the city dispatched an additional election inspector to the community center to ensure that such an incident does not occur again.

Voters in Ward 1, Precinct 4, which includes most of the Kerrytown neighborhood, cast their ballots at the community center.

Paul Fulton, an election official working at the polls, said the malfunction halted voting for about 20 minutes. Fulton added that no votes would be affected and every ballot cast at the Community Center would be counted.

Rackham student Zach Renner waited in line for more than an hour and a half to vote. He said he expected a large crowd at the polling place, but he said he didn't think he'd have to wait for such an extended period of time. Still, he said he was glad he waited.

"I intend to vote," he said. "It'll be just as bad after work hours."

Though voters said they weren't provided with much information, people were waiting patiently and talking with one another.

"We're all determined to vote," Rackham student Adam Kriesberg said.

The Ann Arbor City ClerkÕs Office could not be immediately reached for comment.
